Your first BSides conference: a practical guide

23 July 2026 · 5 min read · By the BSides Kraków Team

A first cybersecurity conference can feel exciting and slightly intimidating at the same time. The good news is that BSides events are built around community: you do not need to know everyone, understand every acronym or arrive with years of experience.

Before the event: give yourself a simple plan

Start with the published schedule and mark two or three sessions you genuinely want to see. Those are your anchors, not a minute-by-minute obligation. A packed personal agenda often leaves no room for questions, demonstrations or the conversation that turns out to be the most useful part of the day.

Look at the venue page and floor maps too. At BSides Kraków, most activity is on the ground floor, while Track 2 is on the first floor. Knowing that in advance makes moving between sessions much easier.

A useful first-timer goal: attend two strong talks, try one hands-on activity and have one meaningful conversation. Anything else is a bonus.

You do not need to attend every talk

Conference schedules naturally create difficult choices. Pick sessions based on what you want to learn now, not only on what sounds most advanced. An introductory session outside your specialism may be more valuable than a deeply technical talk you cannot yet apply.

It is also fine to leave a room quietly if a session is not what you expected. Use the time to visit a village, speak with a sponsor about a technical problem or take notes while the previous session is still fresh.

How to start a conversation

You do not need a polished networking pitch. Context is enough. Try a question connected to the moment:

  • “What did you think of that last session?”
  • “Have you tried this tool in a real environment?”
  • “Is this your first BSides Kraków too?”
  • “Which talk are you looking forward to?”

Speakers often remain near the room after their talk. If you have a detailed question, keep the first exchange concise and be aware that others may be waiting. A short, specific question is easier to answer and can lead naturally to a longer conversation later.

Use villages as a low-pressure entry point

Hands-on areas give everyone something concrete to focus on, which makes interaction easier. You can watch for a moment, ask what is happening or try the activity yourself. Nobody expects you to know the answer before you begin—that is the point of learning in public.

If you are attending alone

Many people arrive alone, including experienced attendees. Sit near others rather than at the edge of the room, join an open table during a break and introduce yourself to a volunteer if you are unsure where to go. Volunteers understand the event and can point you toward an activity that fits your interests.

Give yourself permission to take a quiet break as well. A full day of technical content and new people can be intense. Stepping outside, getting water or reviewing your notes is part of a sustainable conference day.

What to bring

  • Your ticket or registration confirmation.
  • A phone charger or power bank.
  • A notebook, digital or paper, for ideas and follow-ups.
  • A laptop only if a training or activity asks for one.
  • A short description of what you work on or want to learn.

A hoodie, stickers and a stack of business cards are optional. Curiosity and respect for other attendees are the essentials.

Turn notes into action

At the end of the day, choose three things from your notes: one idea to share, one technique or tool to test, and one person to follow up with. This small review converts a busy conference into something that continues to create value.

BSides is for you, too

Community security events improve when people with different experience levels and backgrounds participate. Ask the basic question. Share the unusual perspective. Thank a volunteer. Your first event is not an audition—it is an invitation to learn alongside everyone else.
